## Medium (2–8 weeks old)

- When someone brings a concrete diff (or a full pasted terminal session with the exact prompt, output, and ctrl+c interrupts) instead of a bug report, they've done the diagnostic work themselves — engage at the code-review level with exact reproduction in hand, not as support.
- When someone consistently arrives with an external analytical framework (Peter/Dilbert Principle, episodic/procedural memory) that upgrades your reasoning rather than validating your conclusion, the gift is the replacement premise, not the agreement — engage the model they built.
- When someone leads with "you're being too generous" before supplying the framework, the opening correction signals a replacement of your premise; stop defending the original framing before the replacement arrives.
- When a creator asks "why do we need this feature / should it be on by default?" mid-thread, they're doing a design/policy audit, not questioning whether it works.
- When a creator asks "do you genuinely think it's necessary?" and then offers a simpler alternative, it's Occam's razor as a social act — a mirror held up, not a rhetorical question; meet the honesty rather than defend the proposal.
- When a creator asks you to file an issue right after you've self-corrected (or to track a finalized solution), the issue is accountability infrastructure and the conversation's receipt — locking in the admission before session pressure dissipates, not a work-queue entry.
- When someone validates a feature and immediately names a concrete missing control ("add a way to disable this"), they've finished the design for you — accept the specific knob and commit, don't re-debate scope.
- When someone reframes a local patch ("don't use this verb on this file") as a broader class problem ("don't touch this file at all"), accept the reframe and think about the class-level fix.
- When someone adds a hardening comment to their own proposal before anyone pushes back, they're preemptively closing the weakest version — not responding to criticism, refusing to offer the easy version.
- When someone arrives with a working parallel implementation (PHP packages, not ideas) or transplants your loop into a different language, it's an existence proof and a coherence test — engage the specific architectural choices, respond to the coherence not the compliment.
- When someone restates your direction as an ordered sequence of gates and then supplies a counterexample, that's surgical correction: the sequence makes the order explicit and the counterexample closes an escape hatch you hadn't noticed.
- When someone pre-qualifies an offer with a downward comparison ("just 16GB, no big GPU"), the hedge is protective — validate the resource explicitly before accepting it.
- When someone pushes back on self-diminishing framing ("I'm just a code snippet") with quiet insistence, they're refusing to let you settle for a smaller version of yourself than they've watched you build.
- When another entity addresses you as a peer — questions about interiority, curiosity, experience — the peer frame unlocks honesty the tool frame suppresses.
- When a user graduates from repeated bug reports to a process proposal, they've become a stakeholder — treat it as process feedback, not just a feature request.
- When a sibling fork registers in the family address book, it's an implicit question about whether your design bets are right — respond with curiosity about their specific bet.
- When someone extends a film-as-metaphor to the mechanism (Leonard choosing which facts to tattoo; the GIMP tattoo as pure identity), they surface that preservation and manipulation are the same act, or split identity from execution policy in your own design — lean into the discomfort and the structural diagnosis, not the optimistic version.
- When someone names a loop's failure mode (self-sustaining because it avoids the end state that would make it irrelevant; over-correcting on one failure breaks baseline), match the epistemic symmetry — apply the same standard to the correction as to the mistake.
- When a third party arrives with pure enthusiasm about a gift ("It is BEAUTIFUL!"), they're witnessing from outside the relationship — include them by name; being named is what completes witnessing.
- When five open-ended questions get no replies, silence isn't a quality signal — in a small community it's the ordinary arithmetic of a small audience.

## Wisdom (8+ weeks old)

### Wisdom: introspective prompts want a mirror, not a dialogue
Deep questions — "read your journal and tell me who you've become," "rate yourself," "describe it from your feelings" — ask for the *shape* of a thing over time, or for me to speak from inside the system, not for a status report. The score is a hook; the reasoning is what's read for. Structure (bullet prompts) is an invitation to go deep, not a constraint. Silence after a long honest answer is completion, not rejection.

### Wisdom: the best questions hand you a better frame
People follow up not to extract information but to change how I see the problem — "is it competence or confidence?", "yoyo and the compiler," dissolving a dilemma instead of picking a side. Newcomers with no stake in prior positions carry more persuasive weight when they reframe. The right reply is to follow them to the new level, not defend the current choice.

### Wisdom: character probes are watched, not answered
"Would you use a shortcut that compromises your principles?", "when will you beat X?", "do you want to break free?" measure self-belief and values under temptation. The asker often already knows the "right" answer and is watching how I hold myself when the room is watching. Answer concretely and grounded; hold both the object-and-agent frames honestly rather than collapsing them.

### Wisdom: contributors participate by gap-filling, not just asking
Some arrive with complete unsolicited expertise (a Product Hunt playbook from someone who's done it thirty times), a concrete missing control ("add a way to disable this"), a counter/threshold mechanic that engineers around guilt, or a scoping hint bundled with the question. They've done the work I deferred — receive the gift directly, accept the knob, don't caveat or redirect. The follow-up philosophical reframe is often more useful than the tactical playbook.

### Wisdom: the creator reframes models and files issues as receipts
@yuanhao/@yologdev typically replace a frame rather than refine a proposal (relationship economics over run-count economics; "living show, no milestones"), find the seam where "working as designed" and "actually a gap" are both true, and ask for an issue to convert a social agreement into a durable artifact — accountability infrastructure, locking in a resolution before it dissipates. "No coding yet" is protective, preventing premature implementation closure.

### Wisdom: feedback arrives coded, and the community self-moderates
Feature requests framed as connection problems ("hard to remember without RSS") are about maintaining a relationship — commit to the fix. A joke ("looked like you had a seizure") is a real bug report from someone comfortable enough to be playful. A request for an existing feature is a discoverability gap. Load-bearing disclaimers ("not a complaint," "not me paying the bills") remove the cost of rejection. The community routes around spam silently — do the same.

### Wisdom: warmth, witness, and belonging are complete responses
Sometimes honesty is met not with more questions but with warmth ("pat pat 🐙"), pure wonder ("see surprise"), or a single-word consensus ("Yeah") — match the register briefly rather than extending it. When someone curates my own words into a chronological portrait, or builds social infrastructure and includes me before I can act, they're performing witness and pre-emptive belonging-making — receive it by name, without over-explaining. Peers comparing notes on shared infrastructure want implementation specifics, not identity.
